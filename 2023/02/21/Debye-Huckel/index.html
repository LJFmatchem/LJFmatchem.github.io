Debye-Hückel Theory

Posted on 2023-02-21 Edited on 2023-03-26

Introduction

If we want to quantify the properties of the electrolyte, there are three interaction we need to concern:

  1. Ion-solvent interaction
  2. Ion-ion interaction
  3. Solvent-solvent interaction

The interaction between ions is a multi-body problem (Superhard to predict). Besides, the interaction between ions and solvents is a very complex process, and even in the absence of ions, the structure of pure solvents is complicated.

The adverse effects of the first two points can be infinitely reduced by diluting the solution, and the third point can be avoided by asking appropriate questions.

But what is the exact degree of fully diluted electrolyte? There is a crucial hint relating to activity coefficient and ionic strength. In dilute electrolytes, these two parameters follow. We can see that the mean activity factor of ions is only to do with the charge of ions and concentration . In that case, we neglect excessive properties of ions, such as electron configuration.

We can construct an ideal system with the following rules:

  • Electrolyte completely ionized
  • Ions are charged spheres without polarization
  • There is only Coulomb interaction between ions
  • The ion interaction energy is significantly less than the thermal kinetic energy
  • Ignore the change of dielectric constant caused by adding electrolyte

For non-associated electrolytes, all these conditions can be infinitely approached by diluting the electrolyte. Further, why should thermal kinetics be introduced? Thermal motion is an essential part of the problem of electrolyte solution because, without it, ions and molecules will rest in the configuration with the lowest energy. Specifically, thermal kinetics implies that the theory to be developed must be statistical.

If the distribution of ions is known, it is possible to calculate the electric potential arising from this distribution. But the calculation of the distribution requires the use of the electrical potential. Debye and Hückle founded the modern theory in 1923. Numerous workers have refined their treatment, including Bjerrum, Onsager, and Falkenhagen.

For a volume unit in the solution, considering the distribution of the ions: the result must be trivial because there is no reason for one point in space to be different from another, and the ions' distribution is uniform in terms of the macro. How to make one point different from another? How to define a valuable ion distribution? One point is different from the other only because of the electrostatic effects.
